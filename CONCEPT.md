# Cognitive Bias App — Concept Sketch

> **Origin:** Brad's been fascinated by cognitive biases for years. Almost started a YouTube channel about them. The hook isn't expertise — it's genuine curiosity about why humans think the way they think.
>
> **Tone:** "I'm not a psychologist. I'm just someone who can't stop noticing these patterns."

---

## The Core Idea

A beautifully designed app that helps people notice their own irrational thinking — not through lectures, but through stories, daily practice, and self-reflection.

Think of it as a **mirror for your brain**.

Not "here are 188 cognitive biases memorised from Wikipedia." More like "here's one thing your brain does that you probably didn't realise, told through a story you'll recognise from your own life."

---

## What Makes This Different

There are cognitive bias lists everywhere. Posters, infographics, Wikipedia pages. But knowing the list doesn't change behaviour. Recognising the bias *in the moment* does.

This app isn't a reference guide. It's a daily practice.

**The shift:** From "I know what anchoring bias is" → "I just caught myself doing it when I was negotiating my car insurance."

## How It Works

### Daily Bias Card

Every morning, you get one bias. But not like a flashcard. More like a short story.

**Example — Sunk Cost Fallacy:**

> *You're 90 minutes into a film you're not enjoying. Your partner asks if you want to switch to something else. You say "no, we've already invested this much time." But the 90 minutes are gone regardless of what you do next. The only question that matters is: will the next 60 minutes be better spent here or somewhere else? Your brain doesn't want you to think about it that way.*

Then a one-line definition. Then a question:

> *When was the last time you stuck with something just because you'd already started?*

No judgement. No "you should do this instead." Just a mirror.

### Spot the Bias (Interactive)

Short scenarios — 3 or 4 sentences — drawn from everyday life. Not business case studies. Real stuff.

> *You're buying a laptop. The first one you look at is £1,899. The second is £1,299. The second feels like a bargain. Is it?*
> 
> **What's happening here?**
> a) Anchoring bias
> b) Framing effect  
> c) Bandwagon effect

These are quick. 30 seconds each. You do 3-5 a day. It builds pattern recognition without feeling like studying.

### Your Bias Journal

This is the heart of it. A private space where you log moments you caught yourself:

> *March 19 — Caught myself in confirmation bias. Was researching whether to buy the Mac Mini and only reading positive reviews. Stopped and deliberately searched for complaints.*

Over weeks and months, you start seeing YOUR patterns. Which biases you're most susceptible to. That's powerful self-knowledge.

### The Collection

A beautiful, browseable library of every bias covered. Grouped not academically, but by life context:

- **Money & Spending** — anchoring, sunk cost, mental accounting, endowment effect
- **Relationships & People** — halo effect, attribution error, in-group bias, projection
- **Decisions & Choices** — status quo bias, analysis paralysis, decoy effect, choice overload
- **Work & Career** — Dunning-Kruger, impostor syndrome, authority bias, planning fallacy
- **Beliefs & Identity** — confirmation bias, backfire effect, cognitive dissonance, Baader-Meinhof
- **Memory & Perception** — hindsight bias, availability heuristic, peak-end rule, spotlight effect

Each one unlocks after it's been your daily card, so there's a sense of progression.

## The Experience

### Morning (2 minutes)
- Daily bias card appears
- Read the story
- Think about the reflection question
- Optionally journal a quick note

### Throughout the Day (30 seconds here and there)
- "Spot the Bias" challenges via notification (opt-in)
- Quick, fun, gamified

### Evening (1 minute, optional)
- "Did you notice any biases today?"
- Quick journal entry
- Streak tracking (but gentle, not Duolingo-aggressive)

### Weekly (5 minutes)
- "Your Week in Biases" — which ones you spotted, patterns forming
- One deeper dive article or video on a bias you encountered

## Design Vibe

- Clean, minimal, lots of white space
- Warm but not cutesy — this is for adults who think
- Illustration style: simple, hand-drawn-feeling sketches for each bias (think: The School of Life aesthetic)
- Dark mode option (obviously)
- No gamification overload — no coins, no XP, no leaderboards competing with strangers
- Personal streaks and progress, not competition

## The YouTube Connection

This is where Brad's original idea comes back in. The app and the channel feed each other:

**YouTube:** Weekly 5-8 minute videos. Brad picks a bias, tells a story, shows how it plays out in real life. Conversational, not scripted-feeling. Think casual, sitting-at-a-desk energy.

**App:** Each video has a corresponding deeper experience in the app. Watch the video on anchoring bias, then spend the week noticing it with the app's help.

**The flywheel:**
- YouTube drives app downloads
- App creates daily habit (retention)
- App insights create content ideas for YouTube
- Community shares create organic reach

## Monetisation (Keeping It Simple)

**Free tier:**
- Daily bias card
- 3 Spot the Bias challenges per day
- Basic journal (last 7 days)

**Premium (£3.99/month or £29.99/year):**
- Full journal history and export
- Unlimited challenges
- Weekly bias report
- Deeper articles and exclusive content
- Early access to new biases
- No ads (if free tier has tasteful ones)

**Why this pricing:** Low enough that it's impulsive. High enough to sustain. £30/year × 10,000 subscribers = £300K/year. Even 1,000 subscribers = £30K/year. And the YouTube channel grows independently.
